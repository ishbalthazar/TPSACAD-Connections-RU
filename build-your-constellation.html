Why this matters

College isn't just what you take. It's who you take it with.

The thesis of Connections are Everything is simple and a little uncomfortable: students who thrive in college don't just get good grades. They build a small constellation of people (peers, professors, and staff) who notice them, push them, and open doors.

The good news is that this constellation is buildable. You don't need to be charming, well-connected, or already certain about your major. You need to be willing to show up to one office, one conversation, one experience. Then another. Then another.

This site is two things at once. The first half is about the people already on this campus who are paid (and often eager) to know you. The second half is about the experiences, what the research calls High-Impact Practices, that put you in close enough range of those people that real relationships can form.

Part II ⋆ Your people

Three kinds of people. One you.

The authors argue that thriving students cultivate three kinds of relationships in college. Here's what each looks like at Rutgers-Newark, and where to actually find them.

i.

Peers

Your future classmates

The students sitting next to you in class are the future colleagues, collaborators, and friends most likely to shape your career. The book's advice: stop waiting to be approached.

  • Raiderlink Browse 100+ student organizations. The fastest way to find people who care about what you care about.
  • Honors Living-Learning Community HLLC. A residential cohort built around social-justice scholarship.
  • Honors College Apply or learn more. Interdisciplinary cohort with built-in research mentors.
  • Office of Student Life & Leadership Get involved. They'll help you start a club if yours doesn't exist.
ii.

Professors

Your would-be mentors

The single most underused resource in college: office hours. Professors are required to hold them, often sit there alone, and almost always remember the students who come.

  • Your syllabus Every syllabus lists office hours. That's the door. You don't need an appointment or a "real" reason. "I wanted to introduce myself" is enough.
  • Path2Success RU-N's roadmap from first year through senior year, with prompts for when to talk to whom.
  • Email template Use the template below. It removes 90% of the awkwardness of reaching out cold.
iii.

Staff

Your campus navigators

The people who actually know how the university works. Advisors, counselors, career staff. They handle hundreds of students and almost never get visited by ones who aren't in crisis.

  • Academic Advising Find yours. Assigned to you when you enroll. Required check-in once a year, recommended once a semester.
  • Career Resources & Exploration CRE in Engelhard 101. Résumé reviews, career fairs, mock interviews, free professional clothing through CareerThreads.
  • RU-N Counseling Center Free & confidential. Short-term counseling, group therapy, psychiatric services for any RU-N student.
  • P3 Collaboratory In Dana Library. Runs Mentoring Matters and connects students to faculty mentors across the university.
Tool ⋆ Copy & paste

Email template: reaching out to a professor for the first time

The hardest email you'll write all semester. This works for office hours, research opportunities, or just an introduction.

Subject: Student in your [course / dept], quick question

Dear Professor [Last Name],

I'm [Your Name], a [year] majoring in [major]
at Rutgers-Newark. I'm currently taking your [course], and
[one specific thing: a reading, a lecture moment, a question]
made me want to learn more.

Would it be okay if I came to your office hours on
[day from syllabus]? I'd love to [ask about / hear
your thoughts on] [topic].

Thank you for your time.

Best,
[Your Name]
[RUID, if relevant]

Part III ⋆ Your accelerators

The fastest way to a constellation: do hard things together.

Decades of education research point to a small set of experiences (High-Impact Practices, or HIPs) that consistently change students' lives. The reason isn't magic. It's that these experiences put you in sustained, real-stakes contact with a small group of people for long enough that relationships actually form. Here's where each one lives at RU-N.

High-Impact Practice How it builds your constellation Where to access it at RU-N
Learning communities A built-in cohort that takes courses together over a year or more. The closest thing to a guaranteed friend group on a commuter campus. HLLC hllc.newark.rutgers.edu
Honors College honors experience
Undergraduate research One-on-one time with a faculty mentor over a real project. The single most reliable way to turn a professor into a long-term reference and advocate. Honors College senior thesis honors thesis program
Department-based research Ask any professor whose work interests you. Most have lab or project openings.
Internships & jobs A workplace gives you a built-in cast of supervisors and co-workers. The book's point: every internship is also a mentor-finding mission. Handshake (RU-N) log in with NetID
Scarlet Service Internship civic-engagement program
Service learning Course-credit work in the Newark community. RU-N's Anchor Mission puts these experiences at the center of campus life, not the edge. Office of Global Initiatives & Experiential Learning globalexp.newark.rutgers.edu
Study away Two weeks abroad with the same eight people creates relationships you can't get in a 14-week semester. Often more affordable than students assume. RU-N Global Initiatives embedded & intercession programs
Rutgers Global full study-abroad portal

If you only do one thing

Start with one.

The authors open the book with this argument and close with it: every great college constellation began with a single person reaching out. Pick one this week.

No. 1, This week

Email a professor.

Use the template above. Pick the class you find most interesting, find their office hours on the syllabus, and send the message. It takes ten minutes and changes nothing if it goes badly.

No. 2, This month

Visit one office.

Career Resources & Exploration, your academic advisor, the Counseling Center, or the P3 Collaboratory. Walk in. Ask what they do. You'll be surprised how much.

No. 3, This semester

Sign up for one HIP.

An internship via Handshake, a study-away program through Global Initiatives, a service-learning course, or a research role with a professor. One experience is enough to start.
